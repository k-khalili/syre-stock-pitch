Section 01: The Setup

The IBD Treatment Gap

3.1 million Americans live with inflammatory bowel disease. That includes ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease. The global IBD therapeutics market reached $63.9 billion in 2026 and is projected to exceed $149.9 billion by 2036.

The treatments are not keeping pace. The best available biologics achieve sustained clinical remission in only 30 to 40 percent of patients at one year. That means 60 to 70 percent of IBD patients fail their first-line therapy. Many cycle through two or three drugs before running out of options.

Why the Ceiling Hasn't Moved

The problem is not effort. The problem is structural. IBD is driven by multiple simultaneous inflammatory pathways. Current biologics each target a single mechanism: anti-TNF drugs like infliximab and adalimumab, anti-integrin drugs like vedolizumab, anti-IL-23 drugs like risankizumab and guselkumab, and JAK inhibitors like upadacitinib. Thirty years of incremental improvements to single-target antibodies have not moved the efficacy ceiling.

The next breakthrough requires a different target. It needs to hit multiple damage pathways at once, not just one.

TL1A: The Structural Answer

TL1A, known scientifically as TNFSF15, is a cytokine that operates upstream of both mucosal inflammation and intestinal fibrosis. TNF blockers miss fibrosis entirely. IL-23 inhibitors miss TL1A-driven amplification. Blocking TL1A addresses both tissue damage pathways simultaneously.

This is not a speculative mechanism. Human genetics confirm causality. TNFSF15 polymorphisms are among the most reproducible IBD susceptibility loci in genome-wide association studies. The gene was not engineered into the disease; it was discovered there.

The pharmaceutical industry has validated the target with capital. Three companies collectively spent $19.4 billion to access TL1A: Merck paid $10.8 billion for Prometheus, Roche paid $7.1 billion for Telavant, and Sanofi paid $1.5 billion for Teva's duvakitug. All three deals were for first-generation, monthly-dosing anti-TL1A antibodies. Spyre's platform is quarterly.

Section 02: The Discovery

What TL1A Is and Why Spyre Is the Heir

TL1A was identified as an IBD risk gene through genome-wide association studies. TNFSF15 variants are consistently linked to both ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease across populations. The cytokine binds two distinct receptors: DR3 on T cells, which drives Th1 and Th17 inflammation, and HVEM on fibroblasts, which drives fibrosis. No other validated IBD target simultaneously addresses both the inflammatory and fibrotic components of disease.

That dual mechanism matters. Fibrosis is what causes irreversible bowel damage in Crohn's disease. Anti-TNF drugs do not touch it. TL1A blockers do.

The Prometheus Legacy

Prometheus Biosciences built the first anti-TL1A to show clinical efficacy in IBD. Their drug, tulisokibart, produced a 26% clinical remission rate versus 1% placebo in the ARTEMIS-UC trial. That signal was strong enough for Merck to acquire the company for $10.8 billion, before tulisokibart entered Phase 3.

The scientists behind that result are now at Spyre. Deanna Nguyen, MD, serves as SVP of Clinical Development. She ran the clinical program that made tulisokibart a $10.8 billion target. MiRa Huyghe serves as SVP of Development Operations. She ran trial execution at Prometheus. Brian Connolly serves as CTO. He was VP of CMC at Prometheus and, before that, at Genentech.

They left Prometheus knowing exactly what tulisokibart could not do. Monthly injections limit patient convenience. A conventional antibody half-life caps how long suppression is sustained. They built Spyre to fix those problems.

The YTE Engineering Platform

Spyre's molecules use YTE Fc modification. This is a set of three point mutations: Met-252-Tyr, Ser-254-Thr, and Thr-256-Glu. Together they increase FcRn recycling, which extends antibody half-life by approximately 3 times.

The same modification is applied independently to each asset in the pipeline. SPY001, the anti-α4β7, reaches a half-life above 90 days. SPY002, the anti-TL1A, reaches approximately 75 days. SPY003, the anti-IL-23, reaches approximately 85 days. All three profiles are close enough to enable quarterly co-dosing from a single injection. That is what makes combination therapy feasible. No other company has achieved matched pharmacokinetic profiles across these three targets.

Section 03: The Evidence

Phase 1: Three Programs, Zero Serious Adverse Events

Spyre has completed Phase 1 for all three core assets. The results across 2024 and 2025 are consistent: long half-lives, target engagement, and clean safety profiles.

SPY001 (Anti-α4β7), November 2024

SPY002 (Anti-TL1A IBD), June 2025

Section 04: Platform Optionality

The Platform the Market Isn't Pricing

Spyre has six clinical assets. Every analyst model prices roughly one of them.

  • SPY001 (anti-α4β7): The next-generation vedolizumab. Phase 2 in UC and CD. Half-life above 90 days.
  • SPY002 (anti-TL1A): The next-generation tulisokibart. Phase 2 in UC and CD. Half-life approximately 75 days.
  • SPY003 (anti-IL-23): The next-generation risankizumab. Phase 2 in UC and CD. Half-life approximately 85 days.
  • SPY072 (anti-TL1A, rheumatology): Phase 2 proof-of-concept in RA, PsA, and axSpA. Enrollment completed March 2026, ahead of schedule.
  • SPY120 (α4β7 + TL1A combination): Preclinical data shows superiority over monotherapy in TNBS colitis models.
  • SPY230 (TL1A + IL-23 combination): Targeting complementary pathways.

The Combination Moat

The combination programs are Spyre's structural competitive advantage. Building SPY120 requires two things: an anti-α4β7 antibody with roughly 90-day half-life and an anti-TL1A antibody with roughly 75-day half-life. Those two profiles need to be close enough to dose quarterly from a single injection.

Spyre has both. No other company does. AbbVie has Skyrizi, but not a matched anti-α4β7. Roche has afimkibart, but not a matched anti-IL-23. Merck has tulisokibart, but not a quarterly version. SPY120 is a trial only Spyre can run today.

The Free Option Nobody Models: SPY072

SPY072 targets rheumatoid arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, and axial spondyloarthritis. The combined rheumatology addressable market exceeds $20 billion. Enrollment in the SKYWAY-RD trial completed ahead of schedule in March 2026. Topline data is expected Q3 2026.

Every analyst model assigns SPY072 a value of zero. That is not a conservative assumption. It is an omission. A positive SKYWAY-RD readout would force a full re-rating of the stock.

The Dupixent Parallel

Dupixent targets IL-4 and IL-13. It launched in atopic dermatitis in 2017 at $0.6 billion in annual revenue. By 2023, it had expanded to eight or more indications and exceeded $13 billion per year. The expansion came from platform breadth, not from a single pivotal win.

Spyre's pipeline covers IBD across UC and CD, plus a full TL1A rheumatology franchise. The current $2.1 billion enterprise value prices approximately one indication. The math on anything beyond that is not in the price.

Section 06: Valuation

The Math

The valuation rests on two complementary approaches: a risk-adjusted net present value model and a reverse DCF that works backward from the current share price.

The rNPV Model

The formula: rNPV = (IBD_PoS × IBD_Peak × 4.5× × combo) + (RA_PoS × RA_Peak × 4.0×) + (M&A_prob × $12B) + cash/share. Revenue multiples are drawn from IBD biologics comparables. Cash per share is $756.5M divided by 60.4M shares, which equals $12.53.

Base case assumptions: 55% IBD success probability, $3 billion peak IBD sales, 30% RA success probability, $2 billion peak RA sales, 1.5 times combination premium, 12% discount rate, 25% M&A probability.

Result: probability-weighted fair value of approximately $92.55 per share, representing a 94% premium to the current price of $47.58.

If all six readouts succeed, the M&A window opens. Potential strategic acquirers include AbbVie, Johnson and Johnson, Novo Nordisk, and Lilly. All have precedent for paying $10 billion or more for validated IBD assets.

  • SKYLINE-UC Part A is open-label. Open-label IBD response rates run 15 to 25 points above blinded trials historically.
  • The combination thesis is still preclinical in humans. SPY120 has zero human efficacy data.
  • Burn rate: Spyre spent approximately $169 million in FY2025. Six Phase 2 trials running simultaneously could push FY2026 to $200 to $230 million.
  • Two validated monthly anti-TL1A antibodies may reach market before Spyre has combination data.
  • Cash floor: $756.5M divided by 60.4M shares equals $12.53 per share.
  • SPY072 RA remains a live option even if every IBD program fails.
  • Worst realistic case: approximately $14, which is 71% below the current price.

You risk 71% to make 94 to 215%. The cash is real: $756 million on the balance sheet. The upside depends on how many of six readouts succeed in 2026.
